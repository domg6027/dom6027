📨 DAVAR LECHEM – Yom Rishon, Revi'i 26, 6028 AA

Shalom, Mitspacha!

⭐ THEME

Justice - Preserving Life through Mercy

📘 READING

“He shall flee to one of these cities and live.”
- Devarim (Deuteronomy) 19:5

“Mercy and truth have met together; righteousness and peace have kissed.”
- Tehillim (Psalms) 85:10

“I desire mercy and not sacrifice, and the knowledge of Elohim more than burnt offerings.”
- Hoshea (Hosea) 6:6

🟫 MITZVOT FOCUS

The cities of refuge demonstrate that YHVH's justice is never harsh or reckless. While His Torah upholds righteousness and accountability, it also protects innocent life by ensuring that every matter is examined carefully before judgment is carried out. Mercy is not the absence of justice - it is justice administered with wisdom, truth and compassion.

Through this command, YHVH teaches His people to value every human life. We are called to resist hasty conclusions, to seek the truth diligently and to extend mercy whenever righteousness allows. In doing so, we reflect the perfect balance of justice and compassion found in the character of Elohim.

🩵 REFLECTION

Our daily interactions often present opportunities to choose between condemnation and compassion. While wrongdoing must never be ignored, neither should we be quick to judge without understanding the full circumstances.

May we become people who protect life, uphold truth and extend mercy with wisdom. As we follow YHVH's example, our words and actions become a testimony of His righteous Kingdom in a world that desperately needs both justice and grace.

👐 PRAYER

Heavenly Father, YHVH, thank You for revealing that true justice preserves life while upholding righteousness. Teach us to seek truth with patience, to show mercy with wisdom and to exercise sound judgment in every situation. Guard us from harshness, prejudice and careless decisions, and help us reflect Your compassion without compromising Your holiness. May our lives honour You as faithful servants of Your Kingdom each day.

Baruch YHVH. Emet.

Shalom until tomorrow.
