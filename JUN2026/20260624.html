Shalom, Mitspacha!

⭐ THEME

Justice – Restraint in Punishment

📖 TORAH READING

“If the guilty person deserves to be beaten, the judge shall cause him to lie down and be beaten in his presence, according to his guilt, with a certain number of stripes. Forty stripes he may give him and no more, lest he should exceed this and beat him with many stripes above these, and your brother be degraded in your sight.”
- Devariym (Deuteronomy) 25:2–3

🟫 MITZVOT FOCUS

YHVH’s justice is always measured, purposeful and restrained. Even when punishment is deserved, He sets clear boundaries so that justice never becomes cruelty and discipline never destroys the dignity of the offender.

🩵 REFLECTION

Human nature often swings between two extremes: showing excessive leniency that ignores wrongdoing or imposing punishment driven by anger, revenge or emotion. YHVH's Torah rejects both extremes. His justice is neither permissive nor vindictive; it is righteous, measured and restorative wherever possible.

The command limiting punishment reminds us that every person, even one found guilty, remains a fellow human being created in the image of Elohim. Justice must address sin without stripping away human dignity. When discipline exceeds YHVH's boundaries, it no longer reflects His righteousness but man's uncontrolled passions.

This principle extends far beyond formal judgment. Our words, correction of others and responses to those who wrong us should all reflect restraint. Whether we are parents, leaders, employers or friends, we are called to correct with wisdom rather than humiliation, remembering the mercy YHVH continually extends to us. In doing so, we display His character, where justice and compassion walk together.

👐 PRAYER

Heavenly Father, YHVH, thank You for establishing justice that is righteous, balanced and full of wisdom. Teach us to exercise self-control whenever we correct others, so that our words and actions reflect Your compassion as well as Your truth. Guard us from anger, revenge and harshness, and help us always to uphold the dignity of every person while remaining faithful to Your standards. May our lives bear witness to Your perfect justice and abundant mercy.

Baruch YHVH. Emet.

Shalom until tomorrow.
